Addition Reactions

Grignard Reaction
1912 Nobel · Victor Grignard
Grignard Reaction
Discovered 1900 · Nobel Prize 1912 · Victor Grignard
Organomagnesium halides (Grignard reagents, RMgX) are prepared by reacting alkyl/aryl halides with magnesium metal in dry ether. These nucleophilic reagents add to carbonyl compounds (aldehydes, ketones, CO₂, esters) to form alcohols after hydrolysis. The reaction is extremely versatile for C–C bond formation.
RX + Mg → RMgX (in dry ether)
RMgX + R'CHO → R–CH(OMgX)–R' → R–CH(OH)–R' (after H₃O⁺)
Nucleophilic Addition C–C Bond Formation Dry Ether, Anhydrous

Aldol Addition & Aldol Condensation
Base or Acid catalysed · α-carbon reactivity
Aldol Addition / Condensation
The aldol reaction involves the α-carbon of one carbonyl compound acting as nucleophile toward the carbonyl of another. Base catalysis generates an enolate; acid catalysis generates an enol. The initial β-hydroxy carbonyl compound (aldol product) may dehydrate to an α,β-unsaturated carbonyl (aldol condensation).
2 CH₃CHO →(NaOH)→ CH₃CH(OH)CH₂CHO (aldol)
→(–H₂O, Δ)→ CH₃CH=CHCHO (crotonaldehyde)
Enolate Chemistry C–C Bond Formation NaOH or H⁺/Δ
Cross Aldol (Claisen-Schmidt): Aldehyde + ketone with no α-H → mixed aldol condensation. ArCHO + CH₃COCH₃ → chalcone (ArCH=CHCOCH₃)
+
Wittig Reaction
1979 Nobel · Georg Wittig · Olefin synthesis
Wittig Reaction
Georg Wittig — Nobel Prize 1979
Phosphorus ylides (R₃P=CR₂) react with aldehydes and ketones to replace C=O with C=C. The key advantage is precise control of double bond position (unlike elimination). Nonstabilized ylides give Z-alkenes; stabilized ylides give E-alkenes.
R₂C=O + Ph₃P=CR'₂ → R₂C=CR'₂ + Ph₃P=O
[2+2] cycloaddition C=C Formation THF/BuLi
+
Diels-Alder Reaction
1950 Nobel · Pericyclic [4+2]
Diels-Alder Cycloaddition
Otto Diels & Kurt Alder — Nobel Prize 1950
A [4+2] pericyclic cycloaddition between a conjugated diene (4π electrons) and a dienophile (2π electrons, usually electron-poor). The reaction is concerted, stereospecific (syn addition on both components), highly endo-selective, and forms six-membered rings. The diene must be in the s-cis conformation.
Diene (s-cis) + Dienophile → Cyclohexene derivative
[4+2] Pericyclic Ring Formation Thermal, no catalyst
Endo Rule: Endo transition state preferred due to secondary orbital interactions. Endo product is kinetic; exo is thermodynamic. Electron demand: Electron-rich diene + electron-poor dienophile (EWG) = HDA (Normal electron demand). Inverse: LUMO diene + HOMO dienophile.

Substitution Reactions

+
SN1 and SN2 Mechanisms
Nucleophilic Aliphatic Substitution
SN2 — Bimolecular Nucleophilic Substitution
Concerted, one-step mechanism. Nucleophile attacks backside of electrophilic carbon while leaving group departs. Leads to Walden inversion (inversion of configuration). Rate = k[RX][Nu⁻]. Favoured by: strong nucleophile, polar aprotic solvent, primary substrate, methyl > 1° > 2°. 3° substrates give elimination instead.
Bimolecular Inversion Polar Aprotic
SN1 — Unimolecular Nucleophilic Substitution
Two-step mechanism: (1) Ionization to form carbocation (rate-determining); (2) Nucleophile attacks planar carbocation from either face → racemization. Rate = k[RX]. Favoured by: stable carbocation (3° > 2°), polar protic solvent, weak nucleophile. Rearrangements (1,2-hydride/methyl shifts) possible.
Unimolecular Racemization Polar Protic
+
Friedel-Crafts Reactions
EAS · Alkylation & Acylation
Friedel-Crafts Alkylation
Aromatic ring alkylated by R⁺ (from RX/AlCl₃). Problems: polyalkylation (product more reactive), carbocation rearrangements. Cannot be used with deactivated rings. Conditions: AlCl₃ (Lewis acid) or HF, BF₃.
ArH + RX → Ar–R + HX (with AlCl₃)
Friedel-Crafts Acylation
Aromatic ring acylated by RCO⁺ (acylium ion from RCOCl/AlCl₃). No polyacylation (product deactivated by C=O). No rearrangements (acylium is resonance-stabilized). Product ketone can be reduced to alkylbenzene (Clemmensen/Wolff-Kishner).
ArH + RCOCl → Ar–COR + HCl (with AlCl₃)
+
Finkelstein, Menschutkin & Sandmeyer Reactions
Halide exchange & diazonium
Finkelstein Reaction
Halide exchange: RCl/RBr + NaI (acetone) → RI + NaCl/NaBr↓. Driven by precipitation of NaCl or NaBr in acetone. SN2 mechanism.
Menschutkin Reaction
Alkyl halide + tertiary amine → quaternary ammonium salt. SN2 mechanism. Used to make phase-transfer catalysts and quaternary ammonium surfactants.
Sandmeyer Reaction
Aryl diazonium salts + CuX (Sandmeyer) or Cu powder (Gattermann) → aryl halides. Also gives CN, SCN groups. Critical for synthesizing aryl halides not available via direct halogenation.

Elimination Reactions

+
E1 and E2 Elimination Mechanisms
Dehydrohalogenation · Zaitsev & Hofmann
E2 — Bimolecular Elimination
Concerted one-step: base removes β-H while C–X bond breaks. Requires antiperiplanar geometry (dihedral = 180°). Rate = k[RX][Base]. Strong, bulky bases favour E2 over SN2. Zaitsev product (more substituted alkene) is major with small bases. Hofmann product with bulky bases (t-BuOK).
Anti-periplanar Zaitsev/Hofmann Strong base, Δ
E1 — Unimolecular Elimination
Two steps: ionization to carbocation (RDS), then loss of β-H to base. Rate = k[RX]. Favoured by: 3° substrates, polar protic solvents, weak bases. Zaitsev product major. Competes with SN1. Can involve rearrangements before elimination.
Via carbocation Zaitsev Weak base, polar protic
E1cb: Carbanion intermediate; base removes β-H first, then leaving group departs. Occurs when leaving group is poor (F) or β-H is very acidic. E.g., elimination of β-fluoroketones.
+
Cope, Chugaev & Hofmann Eliminations
Pyrolytic · syn-periplanar
Cope Elimination
Amine oxides (R₃N⁺–O⁻) undergo syn-periplanar, intramolecular, cyclic 5-membered TS elimination at ~100–150°C to give alkene + hydroxylamine. Complementary to Hofmann (gives different regiochemistry).
Chugaev Elimination
Xanthate esters (–O–CS–SMe) undergo cis-elimination via 6-membered cyclic TS at ~200°C. Mild, no strong base needed. Gives alkene + COS + MeSH. Hofmann-type product.
Hofmann Elimination
Exhaustive methylation of amines (3× CH₃I, then AgOH, Δ) → elimination of trimethylamine. Anti-Zaitsev: less substituted (Hofmann) alkene preferred because trimethylammonium is bulky.

+
Rearrangement Reactions — 1,2-Shifts & Sigmatropic
Pinacol, Beckmann, Baeyer-Villiger, Cope, Claisen
Pinacol Rearrangement
1,2-diol (pinacol) treated with H⁺ loses one OH (→ carbocation), then 1,2-alkyl or aryl shift gives pinacolone (ketone). Driving force: more stable carbocation or carbonyl formed.
pinacol →(H⁺)→ pinacolone (methyl tert-butyl ketone)
Beckmann Rearrangement
Oximes treated with strong acids (H₂SO₄, PCl₅, polyphosphoric acid) undergo 1,2-migration anti to the –OH group giving amides (or lactams from cyclic ketones). Used industrially (nylon-6 from cyclohexanone oxime).
R₂C=NOH →(H⁺)→ RCONHR' (amide)
Baeyer-Villiger Rearrangement
Ketones react with peracids (mCPBA, H₂O₂/BF₃) via Criegee intermediate; group migrates anti to departing –O–OH. Migratory aptitude: tertiary > secondary > aryl > primary > methyl.
R–CO–R' + mCPBA → R–COO–R' (ester)
Cope Rearrangement
[3,3]-Sigmatropic rearrangement of 1,5-dienes via chair-like 6-membered TS. Thermally allowed, reversible. Oxy-Cope: 2-hydroxy-1,5-diene gives δ,ε-unsaturated ketone after keto-enol tautomerism.
1,5-hexadiene →(Δ, [3,3])→1,5-hexadiene (interconversion)
Claisen Rearrangement
Allyl vinyl ethers undergo [3,3]-sigmatropic rearrangement. Aromatic Claisen: allyl aryl ethers → ortho-allyl phenols (via cyclohexadienone). Eschenmoser-Claisen: allyl alcohol + ketene acetal → homoallylic amide.
Allyl vinyl ether →(Δ,[3,3])→ γ,δ-unsaturated carbonyl
Curtius, Hofmann & Lossen Rearrangements
Acyl azide (Curtius) or N-haloamide/base (Hofmann) or hydroxamate (Lossen) → isocyanate via nitrene-like intermediate → amine after hydrolysis. Carbon chain shortened by one.
RCO–NH₂ →(Br₂/NaOH, Hofmann)→ RNH₂ + CO₂

Carbonyl Chemistry — Condensation Reactions
Knoevenagel · Perkin · Reformatsky · Mannich · Henry
Knoevenagel Condensation
Aldehyde + active methylene compound (malonic acid, malononitrile) + amine catalyst → α,β-unsaturated product. Milder version of Aldol. Widely used in dye synthesis.
RCHO + CH₂(CN)₂ →(amine)→ RCH=C(CN)₂
Perkin Reaction
Aromatic aldehyde + acetic anhydride + sodium acetate (base) → α,β-unsaturated carboxylic acid (cinnamic acid type). Mechanism involves acetate enolate addition to ArCHO.
ArCHO + (CH₃CO)₂O →(NaOAc, Δ)→ ArCH=CHCOOH
Reformatsky Reaction
α-Halo ester + Zn (like Grignard but gentler) → organozinc enolate → adds to aldehyde/ketone → β-hydroxy ester after hydrolysis. Mild; doesn't react with esters.
RCOCl + BrCH₂CO₂Et + Zn → β-hydroxy ester
Mannich Reaction
Three-component reaction: active methylene compound + formaldehyde + amine → β-amino carbonyl (Mannich base). Mechanism: iminium ion formation → electrophilic attack by enol/enolate.
RCOCH₃ + HCHO + R₂NH → RCOCH₂CH₂NR₂
Henry (Nitroaldol) Reaction
Nitroalkane (base-activated α-carbon) + aldehyde → β-nitroalcohol. The nitro group can be reduced to amine (Nef reaction) or eliminated to nitroalkene.
RCHO + CH₃NO₂ →(base)→ RCH(OH)CH₂NO₂
Claisen Condensation
Two esters + NaOEt → β-ketoester. Requires at least one ester with α-H. Mixed Claisen: ester + ester (no α-H) → selective condensation. Intramolecular: Dieckmann cyclization.
2 CH₃CO₂Et →(NaOEt)→ CH₃COCH₂CO₂Et (ethyl acetoacetate)
