Alkanes — Saturated Hydrocarbons (CₙH₂ₙ₊₂)
sp³ · σ bonds only

Alkanes contain only C–H and C–C single bonds. They are the least reactive class of organic compounds. Their reactions are primarily free radical halogenation and combustion.

+
Alkenes — Unsaturated Hydrocarbons (CₙH₂ₙ)
sp² · Contains C=C π bond

The C=C double bond makes alkenes much more reactive than alkanes. The π electrons are targets for electrophilic addition reactions. Markovnikov's rule governs regioselectivity.

Electrophilic Addition: HX (Markovnikov), H₂O (acid-catalysed), Br₂ (anti addition), H₂ (syn addition/catalytic hydrogenation), HOX (halohydrin formation)
Oxidation Reactions: Epoxidation (mCPBA), Dihydroxylation (OsO₄), Ozonolysis (O₃), Wacker Oxidation (PdCl₂)
CH₂=CH₂ + HBr → CH₃–CH₂Br (Markovnikov addition)
CH₂=CH₂ + Br₂ (CCl₄) → BrCH₂–CH₂Br (anti addition)
+
Alkynes — Triple Bond Hydrocarbons (CₙH₂ₙ₋₂)
sp · Contains C≡C

Terminal alkynes (RC≡CH) have weakly acidic terminal H (pKa ≈ 25) and form acetylide anions with strong bases. React via electrophilic addition (twice) or nucleophilic addition.

Key Reactions: Hydration (H₂SO₄/HgSO₄ → ketone via Markovnikov), Hydrohalogenation (2× HX), Halogenation (2× X₂), Lindlar hydrogenation (→ cis alkene), Na/NH₃ reduction (→ trans alkene), Alkylation of terminal alkynes
RC≡CH + NaNH₂ → RC≡C⁻Na⁺ + NH₃ (acetylide formation)
+
Aromatic Compounds — Benzene & Derivatives
Hückel: (4n+2) π electrons

Aromaticity requires: planar molecule, cyclic conjugation, (4n+2) π electrons (Hückel's rule). Benzene undergoes Electrophilic Aromatic Substitution (EAS) rather than addition to preserve aromaticity.

EAS Reactions: Halogenation (X₂/FeX₃), Nitration (HNO₃/H₂SO₄), Sulfonation (SO₃/H₂SO₄), Friedel-Crafts Alkylation (RX/AlCl₃), Friedel-Crafts Acylation (RCOCl/AlCl₃)
Activating Groups (ortho/para directors): –OH, –OR, –NH₂, –NHR, –NR₂, –NHCOR, –R, –Ar
Deactivating Groups (meta directors): –NO₂, –CN, –COOH, –SO₃H, –CHO, –COR, –CCl₃

Grignard Reaction
1912 Nobel · Victor Grignard
Grignard Reaction
Discovered 1900 · Nobel Prize 1912 · Victor Grignard
Organomagnesium halides (Grignard reagents, RMgX) are prepared by reacting alkyl/aryl halides with magnesium metal in dry ether. These nucleophilic reagents add to carbonyl compounds (aldehydes, ketones, CO₂, esters) to form alcohols after hydrolysis. The reaction is extremely versatile for C–C bond formation.
RX + Mg → RMgX (in dry ether)
RMgX + R'CHO → R–CH(OMgX)–R' → R–CH(OH)–R' (after H₃O⁺)
Nucleophilic Addition C–C Bond Formation Dry Ether, Anhydrous

Aldol Addition & Aldol Condensation
Base or Acid catalysed · α-carbon reactivity
Aldol Addition / Condensation
The aldol reaction involves the α-carbon of one carbonyl compound acting as nucleophile toward the carbonyl of another. Base catalysis generates an enolate; acid catalysis generates an enol. The initial β-hydroxy carbonyl compound (aldol product) may dehydrate to an α,β-unsaturated carbonyl (aldol condensation).
2 CH₃CHO →(NaOH)→ CH₃CH(OH)CH₂CHO (aldol)
→(–H₂O, Δ)→ CH₃CH=CHCHO (crotonaldehyde)
Enolate Chemistry C–C Bond Formation NaOH or H⁺/Δ
Cross Aldol (Claisen-Schmidt): Aldehyde + ketone with no α-H → mixed aldol condensation. ArCHO + CH₃COCH₃ → chalcone (ArCH=CHCOCH₃)
+
Wittig Reaction
1979 Nobel · Georg Wittig · Olefin synthesis
Wittig Reaction
Georg Wittig — Nobel Prize 1979
Phosphorus ylides (R₃P=CR₂) react with aldehydes and ketones to replace C=O with C=C. The key advantage is precise control of double bond position (unlike elimination). Nonstabilized ylides give Z-alkenes; stabilized ylides give E-alkenes.
R₂C=O + Ph₃P=CR'₂ → R₂C=CR'₂ + Ph₃P=O
[2+2] cycloaddition C=C Formation THF/BuLi
+
Diels-Alder Reaction
1950 Nobel · Pericyclic [4+2]
Diels-Alder Cycloaddition
Otto Diels & Kurt Alder — Nobel Prize 1950
A [4+2] pericyclic cycloaddition between a conjugated diene (4π electrons) and a dienophile (2π electrons, usually electron-poor). The reaction is concerted, stereospecific (syn addition on both components), highly endo-selective, and forms six-membered rings. The diene must be in the s-cis conformation.
Diene (s-cis) + Dienophile → Cyclohexene derivative
[4+2] Pericyclic Ring Formation Thermal, no catalyst
Endo Rule: Endo transition state preferred due to secondary orbital interactions. Endo product is kinetic; exo is thermodynamic. Electron demand: Electron-rich diene + electron-poor dienophile (EWG) = HDA (Normal electron demand). Inverse: LUMO diene + HOMO dienophile.

Substitution Reactions

+
SN1 and SN2 Mechanisms
Nucleophilic Aliphatic Substitution
SN2 — Bimolecular Nucleophilic Substitution
Concerted, one-step mechanism. Nucleophile attacks backside of electrophilic carbon while leaving group departs. Leads to Walden inversion (inversion of configuration). Rate = k[RX][Nu⁻]. Favoured by: strong nucleophile, polar aprotic solvent, primary substrate, methyl > 1° > 2°. 3° substrates give elimination instead.
Bimolecular Inversion Polar Aprotic
SN1 — Unimolecular Nucleophilic Substitution
Two-step mechanism: (1) Ionization to form carbocation (rate-determining); (2) Nucleophile attacks planar carbocation from either face → racemization. Rate = k[RX]. Favoured by: stable carbocation (3° > 2°), polar protic solvent, weak nucleophile. Rearrangements (1,2-hydride/methyl shifts) possible.
Unimolecular Racemization Polar Protic
+
Friedel-Crafts Reactions
EAS · Alkylation & Acylation
Friedel-Crafts Alkylation
Aromatic ring alkylated by R⁺ (from RX/AlCl₃). Problems: polyalkylation (product more reactive), carbocation rearrangements. Cannot be used with deactivated rings. Conditions: AlCl₃ (Lewis acid) or HF, BF₃.
ArH + RX → Ar–R + HX (with AlCl₃)
Friedel-Crafts Acylation
Aromatic ring acylated by RCO⁺ (acylium ion from RCOCl/AlCl₃). No polyacylation (product deactivated by C=O). No rearrangements (acylium is resonance-stabilized). Product ketone can be reduced to alkylbenzene (Clemmensen/Wolff-Kishner).
ArH + RCOCl → Ar–COR + HCl (with AlCl₃)
+
Finkelstein, Menschutkin & Sandmeyer Reactions
Halide exchange & diazonium
Finkelstein Reaction
Halide exchange: RCl/RBr + NaI (acetone) → RI + NaCl/NaBr↓. Driven by precipitation of NaCl or NaBr in acetone. SN2 mechanism.
Menschutkin Reaction
Alkyl halide + tertiary amine → quaternary ammonium salt. SN2 mechanism. Used to make phase-transfer catalysts and quaternary ammonium surfactants.
Sandmeyer Reaction
Aryl diazonium salts + CuX (Sandmeyer) or Cu powder (Gattermann) → aryl halides. Also gives CN, SCN groups. Critical for synthesizing aryl halides not available via direct halogenation.

Elimination Reactions

+
E1 and E2 Elimination Mechanisms
Dehydrohalogenation · Zaitsev & Hofmann
E2 — Bimolecular Elimination
Concerted one-step: base removes β-H while C–X bond breaks. Requires antiperiplanar geometry (dihedral = 180°). Rate = k[RX][Base]. Strong, bulky bases favour E2 over SN2. Zaitsev product (more substituted alkene) is major with small bases. Hofmann product with bulky bases (t-BuOK).
Anti-periplanar Zaitsev/Hofmann Strong base, Δ
E1 — Unimolecular Elimination
Two steps: ionization to carbocation (RDS), then loss of β-H to base. Rate = k[RX]. Favoured by: 3° substrates, polar protic solvents, weak bases. Zaitsev product major. Competes with SN1. Can involve rearrangements before elimination.
Via carbocation Zaitsev Weak base, polar protic
E1cb: Carbanion intermediate; base removes β-H first, then leaving group departs. Occurs when leaving group is poor (F) or β-H is very acidic. E.g., elimination of β-fluoroketones.
+
Cope, Chugaev & Hofmann Eliminations
Pyrolytic · syn-periplanar
Cope Elimination
Amine oxides (R₃N⁺–O⁻) undergo syn-periplanar, intramolecular, cyclic 5-membered TS elimination at ~100–150°C to give alkene + hydroxylamine. Complementary to Hofmann (gives different regiochemistry).
Chugaev Elimination
Xanthate esters (–O–CS–SMe) undergo cis-elimination via 6-membered cyclic TS at ~200°C. Mild, no strong base needed. Gives alkene + COS + MeSH. Hofmann-type product.
Hofmann Elimination
Exhaustive methylation of amines (3× CH₃I, then AgOH, Δ) → elimination of trimethylamine. Anti-Zaitsev: less substituted (Hofmann) alkene preferred because trimethylammonium is bulky.

Rearrangement Reactions — 1,2-Shifts & Sigmatropic
Pinacol, Beckmann, Baeyer-Villiger, Cope, Claisen
Pinacol Rearrangement
1,2-diol (pinacol) treated with H⁺ loses one OH (→ carbocation), then 1,2-alkyl or aryl shift gives pinacolone (ketone). Driving force: more stable carbocation or carbonyl formed.
pinacol →(H⁺)→ pinacolone (methyl tert-butyl ketone)
Beckmann Rearrangement
Oximes treated with strong acids (H₂SO₄, PCl₅, polyphosphoric acid) undergo 1,2-migration anti to the –OH group giving amides (or lactams from cyclic ketones). Used industrially (nylon-6 from cyclohexanone oxime).
R₂C=NOH →(H⁺)→ RCONHR' (amide)
Baeyer-Villiger Rearrangement
Ketones react with peracids (mCPBA, H₂O₂/BF₃) via Criegee intermediate; group migrates anti to departing –O–OH. Migratory aptitude: tertiary > secondary > aryl > primary > methyl.
R–CO–R' + mCPBA → R–COO–R' (ester)
Cope Rearrangement
[3,3]-Sigmatropic rearrangement of 1,5-dienes via chair-like 6-membered TS. Thermally allowed, reversible. Oxy-Cope: 2-hydroxy-1,5-diene gives δ,ε-unsaturated ketone after keto-enol tautomerism.

Synthesis of Alcohols — Complete Classification
Functional Group Preparation

Alcohols by Class

Alcohols are classified by the number of carbon substituents on the carbon bearing the –OH group. Each class has distinct preparation methods and reactivity patterns.

Primary 1°
Primary Alcohols (1°) RCH₂OH

Carbon bearing –OH has ONE alkyl/aryl substituent (or none for methanol). Primary alcohols are good nucleophiles, oxidize to aldehydes (mild oxidant) or carboxylic acids (strong oxidant).

Boiling Points: Higher than alkanes due to H-bonding. Methanol (65°C), Ethanol (78°C), 1-Propanol (97°C), 1-Butanol (118°C).

Name Reactions that Prepare Primary Alcohols

Grignard + Formaldehyde
RMgX + HCHO → RCH₂OH (1°) after H₃O⁺. Formaldehyde is the ONLY aldehyde giving primary alcohol from Grignard.
Hydroboration-Oxidation
BH₃·THF adds anti-Markovnikov to terminal alkene → boronate → H₂O₂/NaOH → primary alcohol. Syn addition, anti-Markovnikov.
LiAlH₄ Reduction of Esters/Carboxylic Acids
RCOOH or RCO₂R' + LiAlH₄ (ether, then H₃O⁺) → RCH₂OH. Most direct method for primary alcohols from carbonyls.
Bouveault-Blanc Reduction
Ester + Na (metal) + EtOH → primary alcohol. Historical method before LiAlH₄. Dissolving metal SET mechanism.
DIBAL-H Reduction of Aldehyde
RCHO + DIBAL-H (–78°C, then H₃O⁺) → RCH₂OH. Mild, selective reduction of aldehyde without touching other functional groups.
NaBH₄ Reduction of Aldehyde
RCHO + NaBH₄ (MeOH or EtOH) → RCH₂OH. Mild selective reduction. Doesn't reduce esters, carboxylic acids, or amides.
Oxymercuration-Demercuration (anti-Markovnikov variant)
Terminal alkene + Hg(OAc)₂/H₂O then NaBH₄ → Markovnikov alcohol. For anti-Markovnikov → use hydroboration-oxidation instead.
Fermentation
Glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆) →(yeast/zymase)→ 2 EtOH + 2 CO₂. Industrial production of ethanol (primary alcohol). Zymase enzyme complex catalyses.
Reduction of Acyl Chloride (Rosenmund)
RCOCl + H₂ →(Pd-BaSO₄/quinoline)→ RCHO; then NaBH₄ → RCH₂OH. Rosenmund stops at aldehyde; further reduction gives 1° alcohol.
SN2 on Methyl/Primary Alkyl Halide (NaOH)
CH₃X or RCH₂X + NaOH(aq) → CH₃OH or RCH₂OH. Classic SN2 hydrolysis with inversion. Works best for methyl and primary substrates.
Epoxide Ring Opening (at less hindered C)
Terminal epoxide + LiAlH₄ (or H₂O, base) → primary alcohol at less hindered carbon. Regioselective anti addition.
Meerwein-Ponndorf-Verley Reduction
Aldehyde + isopropanol →(Al(OiPr)₃)→ primary alcohol. Transfer hydrogenation; chemoselective, won't reduce C=C.
Secondary 2°
Secondary Alcohols (2°) R₂CHOH

Carbon bearing –OH has TWO alkyl/aryl substituents. Secondary alcohols oxidize to ketones (not further). They are more reactive toward dehydration and rearrangement than primary alcohols.

Oxidation: 2° alcohols → ketones with PCC, PDC, Jones, Swern, DMP, or even KMnO₄ (no over-oxidation past ketone under normal conditions).

Name Reactions that Prepare Secondary Alcohols

Grignard + Aldehyde (not HCHO)
RMgX + R'CHO → R–CH(OH)–R' (2°) after H₃O⁺. Any aldehyde other than formaldehyde gives a secondary alcohol.
NaBH₄ or LiAlH₄ Reduction of Ketone
R₂C=O + NaBH₄ → R₂CHOH (2°). NaBH₄ in protic solvent; LiAlH₄ in ether. Hydride delivery to carbonyl carbon.
Markovnikov Hydration of Alkene
R–CH=CH₂ + H₂O →(H⁺)→ R–CH(OH)–CH₃ (2° Markovnikov). Carbocation mechanism; gives Markovnikov product (more substituted OH).
Oxymercuration-Demercuration
Alkene + Hg(OAc)₂/H₂O → organomercury → NaBH₄ → Markovnikov alcohol. No rearrangements; anti addition (Markovnikov).
OsO₄ Dihydroxylation (gives diol)
Alkene + OsO₄ (cat.) + NMO → syn-diol (two secondary or one primary + one secondary OH). Both OHs added to same face.
Wacker Oxidation
Internal alkene + O₂ →(PdCl₂/CuCl₂)→ secondary ketone, which can be reduced to secondary alcohol. (Primary: terminal alkene → methyl ketone).
Meerwein-Ponndorf-Verley (Ketone → 2° Alcohol)
Ketone + 2-propanol →(Al(OiPr)₃)→ secondary alcohol. Equilibrium driven by acetone distillation. Oppenauer oxidation is the reverse.
Enzymatic Reduction of Ketones
Ketone + NADPH →(ketoreductase)→ chiral secondary alcohol with high ee. Used in asymmetric synthesis of pharmaceutical intermediates.
Pinacol Coupling
2 RCHO or 2 R₂C=O →(Mg/TiCl₄ or low-valent metal)→ 1,2-diol (pinacol, vicinal diol with two 2° OH). Reductive coupling via ketyl radical.
Sharpless Asymmetric Dihydroxylation
Alkene + OsO₄/K₃Fe(CN)₆ + (DHQ)₂PHAL or (DHQD)₂PHAL → chiral diol. AD-mix-α or AD-mix-β for face selectivity. Nobel 2001.
CBS Reduction (Corey-Bakshi-Shibata)
Ketone + BH₃ →(CBS oxazaborolidine catalyst)→ chiral secondary alcohol. Enantioselective borane reduction. Widely used in pharma.
Tertiary 3°
Tertiary Alcohols (3°) R₃COH

Carbon bearing –OH has THREE alkyl/aryl substituents. Tertiary alcohols CANNOT be oxidized to carbonyl compounds under normal conditions (no α-H). They are most reactive toward dehydration and SN1 reactions.

Lucas Test: 3° alcohols react immediately with ZnCl₂/HCl (turns cloudy/two layers instantly at room temperature) due to fast SN1 via stable 3° carbocation.

Name Reactions that Prepare Tertiary Alcohols

Grignard + Ketone
RMgX + R'COR'' → R–C(OH)(R')(R'') (3°) after H₃O⁺. The most general route to tertiary alcohols. Any ketone + any Grignard reagent.
Grignard + Ester (2 equiv.)
RCO₂R' + 2 R''MgX → R–C(OH)(R'')₂ (3°, symmetric) after H₃O⁺. Ester reacts with first Grignard → ketone intermediate → reacts with second equiv. → 3° alcohol.
Organolithium Reagent + Ketone
RLi + R'COR'' → R–C(OLi)(R')(R'') → 3° alcohol. Organolithiums are more reactive than Grignard; add to even hindered ketones. More basic; less stable.
Grignard + Acyl Chloride (1 equiv.)
RCOCl + R'MgX → ketone intermediate → reacts with second R'MgX → 3° alcohol (if excess Grignard) or ketone (if careful stoichiometry with 1 equiv).
Grignard + CO₂ then another Grignard (indirect)
RMgX + CO₂ → RCOOH → RCOCl → +R'MgX → RCOR' → +R'MgX → R–C(OH)R'₂. Extended route to unsymmetric tertiary alcohols.
Cram's Rule / Chelation-Controlled Addition
α-Chiral ketone + nucleophile: Cram's rule predicts attack on less hindered face (Felkin-Anh model). Chelation control (Mg, Li) can reverse selectivity → stereoselective 3° alcohol.
Corey-Itsuno (Oxazaborolidine) Reduction — note: gives 2°
Note: Tertiary alcohols CANNOT be made by carbonyl reduction (no C–H present). Routes always involve C–C bond formation at C=O: Grignard, organolithium, or organocerium addition to ketones.
Markovnikov Hydration of Disubstituted/Trisubstituted Alkene
R₂C=CHR' + H₂O →(H⁺)→ R₂C(OH)–CH₂R' (3°). Highly substituted alkene → most substituted carbon gets OH (Markovnikov). Fast via stable 3° carbocation.
Organocerium Reagent Addition to Ketone
RLi + CeCl₃ → R–CeCl₂ (organocerium). Adds to enolizable ketones with less enolization side reaction than RLi. Gives 3° alcohol with high yield.
Barbier-Type Reaction
In situ generation of organometallic (Zn, In, Sm) from alkyl halide + ketone in one pot → 3° alcohol. Indium especially useful (works in water).
Reformatsky Reagent with Ketone
α-Bromo ester + Zn + ketone (R₂C=O) → Reformatsky: β-hydroxy ester. If using with tertiary ketones selectively, gives β-hydroxy ester with quaternary carbon.
Phenols Ar–OH
Phenols (ArOH) — Aromatic Alcohols ArOH

Phenols are more acidic (pKa ≈ 10) than aliphatic alcohols (pKa ≈ 16–18) due to resonance stabilization of phenoxide ion. The lone pair on O is delocalized into aromatic ring.

Dow Process (Industrial)
Chlorobenzene + NaOH →(high T/P)→ sodium phenoxide → H⁺ → phenol. Industrial route. SNAr (nucleophilic aromatic substitution) mechanism.
Cumene Hydroperoxide Process
Benzene → cumene (AlCl₃) → cumene hydroperoxide (O₂) → phenol + acetone (H⁺). Main industrial route to phenol. Hock rearrangement.
Diazonium Salt Hydrolysis
ArNH₂ → ArN₂⁺ (NaNO₂/HCl) → ArOH (H₂O/H⁺, boiling). Gives phenol from aniline. Key step: diazonium → SN1 at Ar via phenyl cation or SNAr.
Dakin Reaction
Ortho or para hydroxybenzaldehyde or hydroxyacetophenone + H₂O₂/NaOH → catechol/hydroquinone. Baeyer-Villiger type oxidation in aromatic series.

Reaction Mechanisms & Kinetics
Mechanistic Pathways

Understanding How Reactions Proceed

Mechanisms describe the step-by-step electron flow in reactions. Arrow pushing — curved arrows showing electron movement — is the language of mechanistic organic chemistry.

Nucleophiles vs Electrophiles

Nucleophiles (Electron Donors)
Attack electron-deficient centers. Characterized by availability of electron pairs (lone pairs) or high electron density. Strong nucleophiles: I⁻, RS⁻, CN⁻, R₂N⁻, RLi, RMgX. Weak: H₂O, ROH, RCOOH.
Lone pair donors Negative/neutral
Electrophiles (Electron Acceptors)
Attack electron-rich centers. Characterized by electron deficiency (positive charge, δ+, empty orbital). Strong: carbocations, protonated carbonyls. Common: halogens, Lewis acids, alkyl halides, carbonyl compounds.
Electron acceptors Positive/δ+

Energy Diagrams

SN2 — Single Energy Barrier
One transition state (no intermediate). Rate = k[Nu][RX]. Reaction profile: reactants → single high TS → products. No intermediate to isolate. Concerted bond breaking + bond forming.
SN1/E1 — Two Energy Barriers
Two transition states with a carbocation intermediate (local energy minimum). Rate = k[RX] only. First TS (ionization) = rate-determining step. Intermediate can rearrange or undergo elimination.

Pericyclic Reactions

Woodward-Hoffmann Rules: Thermal [4n+2] pericyclic reactions are thermally allowed; [4n] are photochemically allowed. All pericyclic reactions proceed via cyclic TS with simultaneous bond forming/breaking.
Electrocyclic Reactions
Opening or closing of conjugated polyenes to cyclic compounds. 4n electrons: thermal ring opening = conrotatory; 4n+2: thermal = disrotatory. Example: butadiene ⇌ cyclobutene; hexatriene ⇌ cyclohexadiene.
Cycloadditions
[4+2] Diels-Alder (thermally allowed), [2+2] photochemically allowed, 1,3-dipolar cycloadditions ([3+2]: Huisgen azide-alkyne "click chemistry"). Form cyclic products from two π-systems.
Sigmatropic Rearrangements
[1,j] and [i,j] shifts. [1,5]-H shift: thermal; [1,3]-H shift: photochemical. [3,3] (Cope, Claisen) thermally allowed. [2,3] (Wittig, Sommelet-Hauser) allowed. All concerted, cyclic TS.

Aromatic Electrophilic Substitution (EAS) — Mechanism Detail

+
EAS Mechanism — Arenium Ion (Wheland Intermediate)
σ-complex · rate-determining step
1
Generation of electrophile: Lewis acid activates electrophilic species. E.g., Br₂ + FeBr₃ → Br⁺–FeBr₄⁻ (electrophilic bromine)
2
Rate-determining electrophilic attack (σ-complex): Electrophile attacks aromatic ring → non-aromatic arenium ion (Wheland/σ-complex) formed. π system disrupted at that carbon (sp³).
3
Deprotonation → restoration of aromaticity: Base (or conjugate base of Lewis acid complex) removes H⁺ from sp³ carbon → aromaticity restored. Overall: substitution (not addition) of H by E.
Why not addition? Loss of aromaticity (resonance energy ≈ 36 kcal/mol) makes addition products unstable. Deprotonation to restore aromaticity is strongly favored thermodynamically.

Super Important Summary

Strong Oxidising Agents
KMnO4, Jones reagent, and O3. Use when full oxidation, oxidative cleavage, or benzylic side-chain oxidation is desired.
Mild Oxidising Agents
PCC, Swern oxidation, and Dess-Martin periodinane. Best for alcohol to aldehyde/ketone conversions without overoxidation.
Strong Reducing Agents
LiAlH4 and H2/Pd. Use for difficult reductions such as esters, acids, amides, nitriles, nitro compounds, and catalytic hydrogenation.
Mild Reducing Agent
NaBH4. Selective for aldehydes and ketones, convenient in protic solvents, and usually compatible with many other functional groups.
